## LOGLINE

Two acquaintances meet at a Lake Superior beach one September morning for a cold-water dip. By March, the water is frozen solid — and they can't stop going in. A film about showing up, unexpected friendship, and finding light in the coldest season.

---

## SHORT SYNOPSIS (~120 words)

In September 2025, Marty joins her acquaintance Dawn for a morning dip in Lake Superior. She keeps coming back. So does Dawn. What starts as a casual ritual becomes something neither of them planned for — a daily anchor, a place to bring their grief and their laughter, a friendship that deepens as the water gets colder.

By February, a third woman joins them. By March, they're breaking ice with hammers to get in.

*Dips with the Girl Gang* is a documentary short about mental health recovery, the strange power of ritual, and what happens when you show up for something — and someone keeps showing up too.

---

## LONG SYNOPSIS (~270 words)

Marty is recovering. Three years of injury kept her off the trails that kept her sane, and by the time her body healed, something else had broken. She was out of shape, overweight, depressed — and ashamed of all of it, living in one of the most beautiful places on earth and unable to feel it.

Then she saw a friend's story: Dawn, taking her boys to Lake Superior for a morning dip. She reacted. Dawn replied: *join us tomorrow.* Marty did. And then she came back the next day. And the next.

Dawn has her own reasons for being at the lake. She lost her closest friend, Tara, and the lake is one of the places where that loss lives — and where Tara can still reach her. The dips are grief and joy occupying the same cold water.

As fall becomes winter, the ritual holds. Layers are added. Ice forms on the shore. In February, Karesa joins — new to the area, homesick, looking for something to hold onto. Now there are three of them, breaking ice with hammers and rakes to clear a path in.

*Dips with the Girl Gang* follows one season of morning dips through iPhone footage, GoPro video, and daily selfies that accumulate into something unexpected: evidence of a life being rebuilt, one cold morning at a time. The film is told in Marty's voice — direct, specific, and shaped by the particular clarity that comes from nearly losing something you didn't know you needed.

## DIRECTOR'S STATEMENT (~280 words)

I started filming the dips because I didn't want to forget them. I didn't know I was making a documentary.

I'm a hobbyist. I've made short films for fun — birthday videos, trip recaps, things that live on my phone and make people smile at family gatherings. This is different. This one is about something real that happened to me, and I was living it while I was filming it, which meant I didn't always know what the story was until I was already in the middle of it.

What I know now is that the dips saved me. Not in a dramatic way — no single moment of transformation. Just the slow accumulation of mornings where I showed up, and Dawn showed up, and eventually Karesa showed up, and we stood shoulder-deep in freezing water and talked about our lives and laughed too loud and felt something shift. That line kept moving — the temperature, the ice, the threshold for what we were willing to do — and we kept moving with it.

I made this film because I wanted Dawn and Karesa to see what I saw when I was behind the camera: that what we built together was real, and worth documenting. The scrolling photo archive in the film — hundreds of selfies from September to March — is the truest thing in it. That's what showing up looks like when you stack it all together.

If you've ever found yourself unexpectedly held by a place, or a person, or a ritual you stumbled into by accident — this film is for you. It's for anyone who needed something and didn't know it until it was already saving them.
